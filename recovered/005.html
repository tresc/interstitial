Page 1  ·  Personal hand
I've been keeping this here because it's the only paper I have access to and I don't want to buy a notebook and have to explain what it's for. Nobody reads the back of the forms. I've checked. The Assessor just takes the stack and puts them in the cabinet and that's it. My name is Daniel. I'm thirty-four. I've been playing for two years. The static started around month three, which the Commission said was normal, slightly accelerated. I have a daughter. Her name is — I'm going to write her name down because I want to. Her name is Clara. She's seven. She has a gap between her front teeth and she doesn't care about it yet, which I hope lasts. I'm writing this because I want to remember things the way they actually are. I think the cards change how you remember. Not what you remember. How. The texture of it. I want to have something that was written before, so I can compare.
Page 4  ·  Personal hand, early shift
The colours have started. I knew they would. It's gradual. The sky is the right colour when I look at it directly but in my peripheral vision it's — I don't know how to describe it. Like looking at a photograph of the sky instead of the sky. Correct but without the thing that makes it different from a photograph. Clara came to stay this weekend. She asked why my hair was funny. I said it was just changing colour. She accepted this. Children accept things. I love that about children. I love that about her. I am writing that down: I love that about her.
Page 8  ·  Handwriting beginning to regularise
The emotional situation is as the documentation describes. I want to be precise: I do not feel nothing. I feel the facts of things. I know what I value. When I hold the cards I feel it more directly, which is — I understand now why Players keep playing. It's not that you want the Accrual. It's that the cards are the only place where the thing you know is true about something also feels true about it. That gap is hard to describe to someone who hasn't experienced it. I hope Clara never experiences it. Clara. I'm writing her name again.
