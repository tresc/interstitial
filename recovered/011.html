Dr. Ostroff — You are going to find this before you find the document it describes. This is intentional. We wanted you to have the context before the content. Otto Voss kept a filing cabinet in his bedroom for the last ten years of his life. You know this. What you do not know is what was in it before it was emptied. It was not empty when he died. His granddaughter emptied it. She did not read what she removed. She was in a hurry, the way people are when they are trying to finish grieving and get back to their lives. She put everything in a cardboard box and put the box in her car and drove home and when she arrived and opened the box it was empty. The documents that were in the filing cabinet are not lost. Documents are not lost. They are filed somewhere else. We are telling you this because in approximately four months you will receive something in the mail and we want you to be prepared. Not alarmed. Prepared. There is a difference and we respect your ability to maintain it. The thing you will receive is warm. — The Pale Commission Office of Prior Correspondence
Pale Commission  ·  Office of Prior Correspondence Received: June 14, 2001
Dr. Ostroff received this index card on June 14th, 2001. Four months later, on October 9th, 2001, the Archive received a package containing a manila folder with seventeen documents in Otto Voss's handwriting, dated 1974–1984. They describe what Voss saw when he entered Thorne's room without knocking. The documents are in vault A, folder OV-1974-1984. Three have been transcribed. Dr. Ostroff has been working on the fourth since 2001. The package was warm on arrival. The folder was room temperature. The documents were cold.
