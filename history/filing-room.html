The filing room is the name given in the literature to the underground space discovered by Dr. Thorne on February 14, 1974, in the condemned section of the Centralia mine network. It is the origin point of the Conduit Deck and, by extension, of Interstitial and every documented consequence thereof. The room has not been accessible since the mine access route collapsed in 1981. Its current state is unknown. Its previous state is documented in Thorne's field recorder and, at second hand, in the accounts of three investigators who reached it in the late 1970s.

Physical description

Thorne's field recorder describes a room of moderate size — he estimated roughly the dimensions of a large office — with smooth rock walls, a flat floor of a material he could not identify, and fluorescent lighting that was functional when he arrived and remained functional throughout his visits. He could not locate any power source. He noted this twice, on two separate visits, in a tone that the Archive's transcription team described as less puzzled than resigned — as though he had already accepted that the room would not explain itself.

The room contained four standard four-drawer steel filing cabinets of the type common in American government offices in the 1970s. They were grey. They were full of documents on Thorne's first visit. They were empty by the time later investigators arrived. What happened to the contents in the interim is not documented anywhere the Archive has been able to find.

At the centre of the room, on a table bolted to the floor with bolts of a threading Thorne could not match to any standard specification, were the slabs.

The slabs

The slabs were anthracite coal, mirror-polished to a reflective finish that Thorne described as difficult to look at directly — not because they were bright, but because whatever they reflected seemed slightly wrong. There were between forty and sixty of them; Thorne's notes are inconsistent on the number, which the Archive now recognises as consistent with the count property of the Conduit Deck itself, though it is unclear whether this is cause, consequence, or coincidence.

Each slab was covered in geometric markings. The markings shifted when viewed from different angles — not as an optical illusion, but as though the marks themselves were in slow motion, rearranging into configurations that appeared related to but distinct from what had been visible moments before. Prolonged observation of the markings produced auditory effects Thorne described as "the sound of a very large room becoming quiet" — which the Archive has cross-referenced with the static reported by Stage One Accrual subjects and noted the family resemblance.

The filing cabinet contents

Thorne's field recorder contains no description of what was in the filing cabinets. He mentions reading them, once, in a letter to his sister: "The files are about us. All of us. Not any of us specifically. Do you understand what I mean by that? I don't think you do. I didn't either, at first." He does not elaborate. The letter is the only document in which he references the cabinet contents at all.

Later investigators found the cabinets empty. The drawers were closed but unlocked. The metal showed no signs of forced entry or disturbance. The documents that had been there were simply not there. The investigators described the empty cabinets in their report as "in order" — a phrase that strikes the Archive, in retrospect, as strange, given that there was nothing in them to be in order.

The Voss documents

The seventeen documents written by Otto Vess between 1974 and 1984, recovered in 2001 and held in vault A, describe what Vess saw when he entered Thorne's room during an active work session. Three have been transcribed. They address the transcription process, the slabs as Thorne displayed them, and the quality of attention Thorne brought to the work. The fourth document, which Dr. Ostroff has been in the process of transcribing since 2001, describes something prior to the transcription — something that had to happen first, before the work of translating the slab notation into cards could begin.

The Archive does not know what that something was. The Archive is waiting for Ostroff to finish the fourth document. The Archive has been waiting since 2001.

The room now

The access route to the filing room collapsed in 1981 during a significant subsidence event in the Centralia mine network. No subsequent attempt to reach it has succeeded. The room may still exist, intact, behind the collapsed section. It may have been destroyed in the subsidence. It may be neither of these things.

The Commission's position, offered in a 1995 letter to the Archive, is that the room's current state is "not a matter of immediate concern." The Archive has filed this position. It does not share it. An underground room with no power source, climate-controlled, with bolts of unidentified threading and filing cabinets that were full and then empty, is — the Archive would argue — a matter of some concern regardless of its current accessibility.

Document 010 — the pre-emptive Form 7-C filed by an unknown party in 2019, postmarked Centralia — lists the location of play in Field 31 as "The filing room." Centralia has had no active post office since 2016. The filing room has been inaccessible since 1981. The Archive has noted both facts. It has placed them next to each other in the file and looked at them for a while. It has not reached a conclusion.
