Conduit Deck Zero is the designation given by the Archive to the first Conduit Deck — the deck produced by Dr. Elias Thorne through his fourteen-month transcription of the geometric notation on the Centralia slabs. It is the source from which all subsequent decks descend, directly or indirectly. It is, in the Commission's framing, the original petition. It is, in the Archive's framing, the origin of every documented case of Accrual, including those of the people who built the Archive to document it.

The transcription

Thorne spent approximately fourteen months transcribing the slab geometries onto heavy grey cardstock using India ink and a ruling pen. He worked primarily at night, in his rented room in Otto Vess's house, with the windows covered. Vess's accounts describe the sound of the ruling pen on cardstock as a persistent feature of the household during this period — heard through the wall, through the night, regular as a clock.

The process was not simple reproduction. Thorne was not copying the slab markings directly onto card; he was translating them into a playable form, which required him to determine what the rules of play were and how the markings mapped onto game mechanics. How he determined this is not documented. The rulebook that accompanied the completed deck — written in a clear, bureaucratic hand distinct from Thorne's normal script — describes a game of negotiation and probability management between two or more players. The rules contain several logical paradoxes that Thorne appears to have been aware of and chose not to resolve. The Archive has noted that this choice was either deliberate or inevitable, and has not been able to determine which.

Completion and discovery

Thorne completed the deck in October 1974. He then remained in Vess's house for three further weeks without, apparently, doing anything in particular. Vess described him during this period as waiting — specifically, as a man who has finished a task and is now waiting for something to acknowledge that the task is finished. Then he was gone.

Vess entered Thorne's room to collect overdue rent and found it empty. On the table:

  • One piece of mirror-polished anthracite, approximately the size of a playing card, warm to the touch
  • Conduit Deck Zero, complete, boxed, sealed with grey tape

On the tape, in the bureaucratic hand of the rulebook, not in Thorne's normal handwriting: "For the next player."

The inscription

The phrase "For the next player" has attracted significant scholarly attention, most of it focused on the word next. It implies a previous player — that Thorne did not consider himself the first, despite being the creator of the deck. The Commission's reading: Thorne recognised himself as a conduit rather than an originator, and acknowledged that the Substrate had been playing with human subjects long before he formalised the mechanism. The Archive's reading: the word next is doing something that the word first cannot do, and whatever that something is, Thorne put it there deliberately.

The deck in Archive holdings

The Archive holds what it believes to be Conduit Deck Zero in vault A. The provenance of this copy is imperfect: the deck passed through at least four hands between Vess's discovery of it in 1974 and its acquisition by the Archive in 1993, and the chain of custody is not fully documented for two of those transitions. The Archive cannot confirm with absolute certainty that it holds the original rather than an early copy.

What the Archive can confirm: the deck in vault A shows no wear consistent with fifty years of age. Its card count, on the four occasions the Archive has attempted to enumerate it, has been forty-one, forty-one, fifty-three, and forty-one. The piece of anthracite found with it is stored separately, also in vault A. It remains warm. It has been warm for fifty years. The Archive checks periodically. Every time it checks, it is warm.

The lineage

All known Conduit Decks trace their lineage to Deck Zero, either directly — as copies made from the original — or indirectly, through subsequent generations of copying. The Archive has mapped twenty-two confirmed deck lineages. It estimates, based on known copying rates and the Commission's own distribution patterns, that the total number of existing decks is between sixty and two hundred. It acknowledges significant uncertainty in this estimate. The Commission has declined to share its own count.

Each copying introduces minor variations in specific cards — small differences in the imagery, slight shifts in the notation on card borders — that the Archive uses to trace lineage. No copy is identical to Deck Zero in every particular. No copy is so different that it fails to function. The Archive's current model: the deck has a stable core and a variable surface, like a language that retains its grammar while its vocabulary shifts across generations of speakers.

The Archive has on four separate occasions attempted to play a card from Conduit Deck Zero under controlled research conditions, with full ethical oversight and documented informed consent from the researcher involved. On each occasion, the designated researcher has picked up the selected card, held it, and put it back down without playing it. None of the four researchers has been able to explain why. All four have filed reports. The reports describe the experience differently but share one phrase, which none of the researchers were aware the others had used: each report notes that playing the card felt, at the moment of decision, like something that had already happened.
