The Discarded are the entry tier of the Pale Commission — those who have witnessed the game or been recruited to the Commission's support structure but have not yet played a card. The title is the Commission's own designation, and it is not, within the organisation, intended as a pejorative. The Discarded have been set aside pending evaluation — the word carries its archival sense, a document held in a separate pile while its disposition is determined, not yet filed, not yet discarded in the colloquial sense.

The Archive's assessment: the distinction between "set aside for evaluation" and "discarded" is thinner than the Commission's language implies, and the Commission's choice of this word for its entry tier is either an unfortunate accident or a very precise piece of institutional honesty.

Duties

The Discarded maintain safe houses where play can occur without outside interference, identify individuals with high substrate permeability — those in states of heightened emotional distress, grief, or transition, who appear more readily affected by the Substrate — and preserve and copy Conduit Decks. They manage logistics, run errands, and fill in forms. They fill in a great many forms. The Commission's documentation for Discarded-tier duties runs to forty-three pages and is, by the Archive's assessment, the most thorough description of administrative support work it has ever encountered outside of an actual government office.

Elevation

The Discarded are told they are being evaluated for elevation to The Players. Many wait years. Some are never elevated. Commission documents do not explain the criteria for elevation, the timeline, or why some Discarded remain at this tier indefinitely. When the Archive asked the Commission about this in 2006, the Commission replied that elevation occurred "when the subject is ready." When the Archive asked how readiness was determined, the Commission said the question had been answered. The Archive's position is that it had not been.

The Archive's study cohort includes fourteen subjects who self-identify as former Discarded who left the Commission without being elevated. Their accounts are broadly consistent: a period of useful activity, a growing sense that the evaluation was not ongoing so much as indefinitely deferred, and a departure that was not contested by the Commission. None of the fourteen have been contacted by the Commission since leaving. Several have noted that this absence of contact is, in its own way, unsettling — that being ignored by the Commission feels less like freedom and more like having been filed under a category they cannot identify.
