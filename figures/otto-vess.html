Otto Vess is the only documented case of Stage One Accrual resulting from passive proximity to an active play session rather than direct card contact. He is also, in the estimation of several researchers, the most significant secondary witness in the Archive's holdings — the only person, during the years he was alive, who had seen Thorne work.

Vess was born and raised in Centralia, the son of a miner, and worked in the mines himself until a knee injury in 1961 forced early retirement. He supplemented his pension by renting rooms in his house — "not lucrative but quieter than the alternative." Thorne was not his first lodger but was, by a considerable margin, his most unusual.

In his own accounts — given to investigators after Thorne's disappearance, to Ostroff in 1989, and in the seventeen documents recovered from his filing cabinet — Vess comes across as a man of considerable precision and very little self-dramatization. He did not enjoy what happened in his house in 1973 and 1974. He did not pretend otherwise. He also did not run from it.

The doorway incident

The moment that produced Vess's Stage One symptoms — entering Thorne's room without knocking during an active session — occupies a central place in the literature not because of what Vess could describe about it but because of what he could not. He was in the doorway for less than a minute. He has no memory of what he saw during that minute. This is not repression in the clinical sense; it is structural absence, consistent with targeted application of The Empty Mirror, though Vess maintained he could not have been the target of deliberate play because he entered without warning and Thorne's back was to the door.

What the incident produced was unambiguous: Vess began hearing the static within days. He heard it for the remaining twenty-nine years of his life. He described it in his 2001 interview with Ostroff as:

Ostroff interview notes — 2001 Voss interview, Cincinnati
Something being sorted. Like someone going through a very large collection of something, very quickly, looking for a specific one.

He held a card once — Thorne left one on the kitchen table — and put it down immediately. When asked why: "It felt like being read. I didn't mind being read. I just thought it was polite to know that was what was happening to you before you agreed to it."

Later life

Vess remained in Centralia as the town emptied around him — as residents were relocated, buildings demolished, streets closed over subsidence fissures from the underground fire that had been burning since 1962. He was one of the last to accept relocation, moving to a small house in the adjacent township in 1992 where he lived until his death in 2003.

When the Commission wrote to him in 1991 — following his appearance at their Cincinnati address — Vess responded with a single sentence: "I know what you are and I am not interested, thank you." The Commission did not write again. The Archive considers this the most efficient communication in its records.

He kept a locked filing cabinet in his bedroom for the last decade of his life. His granddaughter does not remember him opening it. After his death she placed its contents in a cardboard box, drove home, and found the box empty on arrival. The subsequent history of those documents is covered in Document 011.

Assessment

Vess is an anomaly in ways that have not been fully resolved. His Stage One symptoms were stable and non-progressive for nearly three decades — unprecedented. Most Stage One subjects either play and progress or cease contact and revert. Vess did neither. He simply carried the static, year after year, as a permanent and non-escalating condition.

He lived eighty-three years in the town where the ground burned. He heard something sorting through a collection he could not name for twenty-nine of them. He did not play. He did not Fold. He died in a small house with a locked filing cabinet full of things nobody read in time.

The Archive considers Otto Vess, quietly and without publishing the assessment, one of the most successful people in its records. To have been adjacent to all of this — to have had it happen in his home, to have heard it for three decades, to have been asked to join it — and to have simply said no and continued living: the Archive does not have a framework for evaluating this. It only has the fact of it. The fact is in the file.
