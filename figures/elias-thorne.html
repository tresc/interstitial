Elias Thorne occupies an uncomfortable position in the literature: he is simultaneously the most important figure in the study of Interstitial and the least understood. Nearly everything known about him comes from secondary sources — the recollections of Otto Vess, a handful of surviving colleagues, three letters to his sister Margaret, and the field recorder recovered from the Centralia mine shaft following his disappearance. His own written work, beyond the Conduit Deck Zero rulebook and the transcription notebooks, amounts to fewer than forty pages of notes that raise more questions than they settle.

What the record establishes is this: Thorne was not a man who inspired strong feelings in people who knew him professionally. His Edinburgh colleagues describe him as competent, methodical, and difficult to read — "the kind of person who always seemed to be working on something other than what he said he was working on, though not in a way you could point to." His failure to secure permanent academic employment was attributed variously to his field's disrepute, to interpersonal difficulties never fully specified, and to what one grant reviewer described as "a tendency to treat conclusions as starting points."

The question of motivation

Why Thorne went to Centralia specifically, and why he went when he did, has been the subject of sustained scholarly attention. The standard account — that he followed his acoustic resonance research to its logical field site — is accepted but not entirely satisfying. The subsonic frequency theory he had developed was speculative and disputed; there was no compelling scientific reason to believe Centralia's underground fires would produce anything his previous research sites had not.

Margaret Thorne, in a 1989 interview with Dr. Ostroff, stated that Elias had told her, in the autumn of 1973, that he had dreamed about a room underground. He described the dream in enough detail that she could still recall it fifteen years later: a room that was the right temperature. Filing cabinets. A table bolted to the floor. In the dream he had already been there before. He went to Centralia, Margaret said, because he wanted to see if the room was real.

It was.

The filing room

On February 14, 1974, Thorne descended into a condemned section of the mine network against explicit written warnings. His field recorder was later recovered. The relevant entry reads, in part:

Thorne field recorder — February 14, 1974 Transcribed; original in vault A
The shaft should have been impassable. There was no collapse. There was a door. Not installed — extruded, I think, is the word. As if the rock had grown into that shape over a very long time. The room beyond was climate-controlled. The lights were fluorescent and working. There was no power source I could locate. The filing cabinets were standard four-drawer steel, grey. They were full. I didn't read the contents for a long time. I stood in the doorway for what felt like four hours. My watch said eleven minutes had passed. In the center of the room, on a table bolted to the floor with bolts I did not recognise as any standard threading, were the slabs.

The filing cabinets' contents have never been formally documented. In a letter to his sister written three weeks before his disappearance, Thorne wrote: "The files are about us. All of us. Not any of us specifically. Do you understand what I mean by that? I don't think you do. I didn't either, at first."

The transcription period

Vess's account of Thorne during the transcription period is the most detailed portrait available. He describes a man who had "decided something and was now only doing what that decision required." Thorne worked at night, kept the windows covered, ate irregularly. He was polite to Vess. He paid his rent. He did not discuss his work.

What Vess noted — and what has been cited frequently — is that Thorne during this period seemed in some way relieved. Not happy. Relieved. "He knew where he was going and he was going there and he had stopped worrying about whether it was a good idea."

The three weeks

Thorne completed Conduit Deck Zero in October 1974. He disappeared three weeks later. What he did during those three weeks is not documented. Vess, when asked, said: "He sat. He played a lot of patience. He was waiting for something, I think, or letting something catch up to him. I didn't ask. You didn't ask Elias things he hadn't offered."

Vess entered Thorne's room to collect rent and found it empty. On the table: one piece of mirror-polished anthracite, warm to the touch. Conduit Deck Zero, complete, boxed, sealed. On the tape: "For the next player."

Status

The Commonwealth of Pennsylvania classifies Thorne as a missing person. The case has been inactive since 1981. The Pale Commission classifies him as "in process" — a designation that appears once in their documentation without elaboration. The Archive has requested clarification on this phrase four times. No clarification has been provided.

The Archive notes that "in process" is not the same as "filed." It does not know if the Commission is aware of this distinction or considers it meaningful.

His sister Margaret, who died in 2012, maintained until the end that her brother was not dead. When pressed, she would say: "He found what he was looking for. I don't know if that's better or worse than what I was afraid of. I've decided to treat it as better."

The Archive has no comment on Margaret Thorne's assessment that adds anything to what she said. It has filed it. It is in the file.
