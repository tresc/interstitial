Yael Ostroff is the founding director of the Interstitial Studies Archive and the author of the field's most cited works. She is also, as of 1989, a Stage Two subject — a fact she has disclosed in her published work, in her course syllabi at Dunmore University, and to anyone who asks. She is not, she has said on multiple occasions, going to pretend that this does not affect her research. She is also not going to stop doing the research.

Ostroff came to the study of Interstitial through folklore studies, where her doctoral work concerned the structural similarities between independently developed ritual game-forms across pre-literate cultures — the persistent emergence, without apparent transmission, of similar symbolic systems in distant and unconnected societies. Her argument, considered uncontroversial within its own narrow field and ignored outside it, was that certain symbolic structures are not invented but found: that they exist in some sense prior to the cultures that articulate them. Interstitial, when she encountered it in 1987, struck her immediately as the most extreme case of this phenomenon she had ever seen.

The 1989 session

Ostroff played The Empty Mirror in a documented session on November 3rd, 1989, with two witnesses present, in a room that had been measured and photographed beforehand. Her notes from the session are published in the 1991 monograph. The entry for the moment of play reads:

Ostroff session notes — November 3, 1989 Published in Foundations of Anomalous Ludic Phenomena, Appendix C
Consistent with theoretical model. Also not entirely.

She has expanded on this in subsequent interviews. The experience, she has said, was not dramatic. It did not hurt. It was more like — and here she has used different analogies at different times — submitting a form and hearing it accepted; finding that a door you had always assumed was locked was simply heavy; turning a corner and seeing something you had been told about your whole life and finding that being told about it had not prepared you at all.

The Stage One symptoms began within a week. The Stage Two transition occurred in 1997. She documented both in real time, in papers her colleagues describe as models of their kind and, quietly, among the most difficult things they have ever read.

Research and methodology

Ostroff's central scholarly contribution is the development of a theoretical framework for the Substrate that is neither the Commission's doctrinal language nor the dismissive skepticism of mainstream academia. She treats the Substrate as a real phenomenon with real properties that can be studied, while maintaining — with what she describes as professional obligation — that studying something does not mean understanding it and does not mean it is safe.

Her relationship with the Pale Commission is complicated. She has received documents from them that she would not have obtained otherwise. She has also been misrepresented in their literature, had her work cited without permission in recruitment materials, and received correspondence she describes as "not threatening but designed to be thought about at three in the morning." She cooperates with them where cooperation produces data. She does not trust them. She has said these two positions are not in contradiction.

The Voss documents

In October 2001, the Archive received a package containing seventeen documents in Otto Vess's handwriting, dated 1974–1984, describing what Vess saw when he entered Thorne's room without knocking. The documents are in vault A, folder OV-1974-1984. Ostroff transcribed the first three shortly after receiving them. She began the fourth in 2001.

She has not finished it.

When asked about the fourth document, she says: "I'll get to it." She has been saying this since 2001. The Archive does not press her. The Archive has, in its own internal documentation, noted that it is waiting. The Archive has also noted that it does not entirely want to know what is in the fourth document, and that this feeling is not consistent with its stated mission, and that it is filing the inconsistency rather than resolving it.

Current status

Ostroff is sixty-nine years old. Her hair has been white since her late forties. Her eyes are described by colleagues as "silver," "pale," and "the colour of overcast sky" — always slightly uncomfortably. She does not discuss her reflection situation publicly. She has said, when asked about Stage Three, that she thinks about it the way she thinks about retirement: something that is coming, that she is not in a hurry toward, and that she intends to face with the same thoroughness she has brought to everything else.

Her handwriting is her own.

The Archive notes this every year, in its annual staff review. It has become, in the estimation of several junior researchers, the most reassuring sentence the Archive produces. It has become, in the estimation of several senior researchers, the most frightening.
