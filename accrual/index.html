Accrual is the term used in academic literature for the progressive physiological and psychological changes observed in long-term players of Interstitial. The process is cumulative, non-reversible, and — according to the Pale Commission's own documentation — inevitable for any player who engages with a Conduit Deck beyond a threshold of use the Commission has declined to specify publicly.

The Commission's preferred term is Phases of Accord, a designation that frames the process as a progression toward something rather than a deterioration. The academic term Accrual — introduced by Dr. Yael Ostroff in her 1991 monograph — implies accumulation: something building up, something being collected. Subjects in advanced stages have, when asked to characterise their condition in their own words, most commonly used the word administrative.

Stages

The Commission's official staging document divides Accrual into four phases. The document was produced in 1987 and has not been revised. Academic literature follows this structure while disputing its framing at almost every point.

Mechanism

The physical mechanism of Accrual is not understood. The Commission's position is that no mechanism in the conventional sense exists — that Accrual is not the body breaking down but the body adjusting to an increasing proportion of its energy being routed through the Substrate rather than through ordinary biological processes. Academic researchers have declined to accept this framing while also failing to produce a competing physical model.

What is documented is a consistent relationship between card use and symptom progression: subjects who play more frequently progress more rapidly through stages. Subjects who cease playing do not regress. Two subjects in the Archive's longitudinal study who ceased play entirely at Stage One have remained at Stage One for periods exceeding fifteen years, which the Commission describes as stasis and the Archive describes as the sole dataset suggesting any degree of manageability.

The handwriting phenomenon

Among the most consistently documented features of Stage Two Accrual is a change in the subject's handwriting not mentioned in Commission public literature and first noted academically by Ostroff in 1994. Across all documented Stage Two subjects for whom before-and-after samples exist, the post-transition hand is identical: a regular, upright, precisely-spaced script researchers have consistently described as the handwriting of a form.

The change is gradual and subjects do not typically notice it themselves. See the dedicated article on the handwriting phenomenon for full documentation, including the anomalous third handwriting identified in Document 008.

The reflection delay

Beginning in Stage Two and persisting through Stage Three, subjects exhibit a measurable delay between their physical movements and the corresponding movements of their reflection in mirrors and other reflective surfaces. The delay begins at approximately 0.4 seconds and may extend beyond one second in advanced Stage Three subjects. The reflection always catches up. It does not deviate from accuracy. It simply arrives late.

See the dedicated article on the reflection delay for measurement methodology and subject accounts.

The question of Stage Four

The Commission designates Stage Four as Completion of Accord and does not classify it as a terminal event. The Archive designates it The Fold and does. This disagreement has been formally noted in correspondence between the Archive and Commission representatives on three occasions. The Commission's most recent response, received in 2019, stated that the Archive's use of "terminal" reflects a misunderstanding of the Substrate's nature that the Commission is available to clarify at the Archive's convenience. The Archive has not yet scheduled the clarification meeting. The Archive is, its editors note, not in a hurry.

The Commission's objection to the term "terminal" rests on the claim that subjects who Fold continue to exist within the Substrate's record. The Archive's position is that continued existence as a static data point in an atemporal field is, from the subject's perspective, functionally indistinguishable from not existing. This discussion is ongoing. It has been ongoing since 1994.
