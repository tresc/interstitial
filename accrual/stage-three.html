Stage Three, designated Crystallisation in academic literature and Structural Integration in Commission documentation, is the stage at which Accrual produces changes to the subject's physical body that cannot be attributed to other causes and cannot be concealed. The Commission's phrase for Stage Three subjects — "the player is becoming legible" — is among the most frequently cited examples of the Commission's characteristic way of describing something alarming in language that makes it sound like an administrative status update.

Dermal patterning

The skin's texture changes gradually over the course of months. It becomes cooler and slightly harder to the touch. In the early stages this may be mistaken for a dermatological condition. As Stage Three progresses, a faint geometric patterning becomes visible — most prominently on the hands, forearms, and neck, though the distribution varies by subject.

The patterning is not random. Across all documented Stage Three subjects, it is consistent in structure with the geometric notation documented on the Centralia slabs by Dr. Thorne in 1974. The scale differs — the slab notation is precise and dense, while the dermal patterning is faint and spread across the body's surface — but the underlying forms are the same. The Archive's current interpretation is that the Substrate is, in some sense, inscribing its own notation onto subjects at this stage. The Commission calls this "integration." The Archive calls it something it has not yet found an adequate word for.

Cessation of sleep and nutrition

Stage Three subjects cease to sleep. Most report this transition as gradual — first sleeping less, then sleeping only occasionally, then sitting at night in a way they describe as serving a similar function. The Commission's documentation describes subjects as "self-sustaining." No biochemical mechanism for this has been identified. Several researchers have noted that the absence of a mechanism does not mean the absence of a cost, and that the question of what Stage Three subjects are running on has not been adequately answered. The Commission has not provided an answer. The Commission has said the question reflects a misunderstanding of the subject's changed relationship to biological process. The Archive considers this a non-answer dressed in technical language.

The cessation of nutritional need follows a similar pattern. Stage Three subjects stop eating, without hunger and without apparent distress. In most documented cases they continue to sit at meals with others for social reasons for some time after the nutritional need has ceased, and stop only when it becomes too conspicuous that they are not eating. This detail — the continued performance of eating for the benefit of others — is noted in several testimonials and has not been commented on by the Commission.

The reflection delay at Stage Three

The reflection delay, which begins at roughly 0.4 seconds in Stage Two, may extend to 1.1 seconds or beyond in Stage Three. The reflection remains accurate throughout — it shows what the subject is doing, correctly, just later. Subjects at this stage who continue to use mirrors describe the experience in similar terms: not frightening, but informative. The reflection is receiving information from somewhere else and rendering it afterward. Where the information is coming from in the interval is not known.

The phrase "registered at an unusual frequency" does not appear elsewhere in Commission medical documentation. The Archive's medical consultant has noted that it is not standard clinical language and does not map onto any known diagnostic category. When the Archive raised this with Commission representatives, they said the phrase was self-explanatory. The Archive's medical consultant does not agree.

Proximity to Stage Four

Stage Three is the final stage before The Fold. There is no documented marker that reliably predicts when a Stage Three subject will progress to Stage Four — no symptom threshold, no measurable value. Stage Three subjects may remain at this stage for months or years. The Commission's documentation offers the following on this point: "The timing of completion is not determined by the subject. It is determined by the record."

The Archive has no comment on this that it considers adequate. It has noted it. It is in the file.
