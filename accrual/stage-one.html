Stage One, designated The Echoes in academic literature and Initial Resonance in Commission documentation, is the first and most populated stage of Accrual. Its symptoms are subtle enough to be attributed to other causes by most subjects experiencing them for the first time, and this misattribution is, the Commission notes in its staging document, expected and within parameters.

Stage One is the only stage at which cessation of play appears to produce genuine stabilisation. Two subjects in the Archive's longitudinal study who ceased play entirely at Stage One have remained at Stage One for over fifteen years without progression. This is the closest the Archive has come to documenting anything resembling good news regarding Accrual.

Symptoms

Auditory static

The defining symptom of Stage One is the onset of a persistent low-level static audible in silent rooms. Subjects describe it variably: "white noise," "something being sorted," "the sound just before someone says your name." The static is not audible on recording equipment. It is not heard by others in the same room. It is present only to the subject.

Otto Vess, who experienced Stage One symptoms for twenty-nine years following a single passive exposure event, described the static in his final documented interview as: "Like someone going through a very large collection of something, very quickly, looking for a specific one." He did not play the game. He heard it anyway. His case remains the most extensively documented instance of passive Accrual in the Archive's holdings.

Colour perception

Subjects report that colours appear slightly muted — not absent or dramatically wrong, but as though viewed through a single layer of gauze. This effect is most pronounced in peripheral vision and tends to resolve when the subject focuses directly on an object. Several subjects have described it as the difference between looking at something and looking at a photograph of that same thing: technically identical, but missing whatever quality makes the original feel present.

The pre-contact reflection anomaly

A statistically significant proportion of Stage One subjects — 73% in the Archive's most recent longitudinal study — report, retrospectively, that they experienced a specific perceptual anomaly before their first contact with a Conduit Deck. In most cases this is a single brief instance in which their own reflection appeared to be standing in a subtly different posture. Not dramatically. Not enough to be certain it was seen. Most dismissed it.

The pre-contact nature of this anomaly has been the subject of sustained academic discussion. The Commission's position is that it demonstrates the Substrate's awareness of the subject prior to formal contact. The Archive's position is that this explanation raises more questions than it answers and that the Commission is aware of this.

Recovered document — player testimonial, 2007 Document 002 — R.V.
Stage One you still feel everything, you just also hear the static and see the colours wrong. It's like having a radio on in another room. You know it's there. You can hear it. You are not the radio yet. The transition is not dramatic. One morning I woke up and made coffee and sat at the table and looked at the cup and knew, very clearly, that I loved my sister. I had always loved my sister. But that morning I knew it the same way I know that the cup is blue. Observationally. As a reported fact about the world.

Stability and the stasis question

The two documented cases of long-term Stage One stabilisation following play cessation represent the most significant evidence available that Accrual has some degree of manageability. Both subjects ceased play after their first game. Both report that the static has not changed in character or intensity in the years since. Neither has experienced pigment changes, emotional flattening, or any other Stage Two marker.

The Commission's documentation does not address play cessation as a strategy. When the Archive submitted a formal inquiry on this point in 2003, the Commission's response was: "The question assumes that cessation is the subject's goal. We have found this assumption to be, in practice, rare."

The Commission is not wrong. Of the 340 Stage One subjects in the Archive's study cohort, only 11 have ceased play voluntarily. 9 of those 11 resumed within two years. The 2 who did not are the subjects referenced above. The Archive has not published the names of these 2 subjects. The Archive notes that it knows their names, and that knowing them and not publishing them is a choice the Archive has made deliberately, and that it is one of the few choices in this area that the Archive feels confident about.

Commission classification

The Commission classifies Stage One as entirely benign and does not require subjects to report their symptoms. This means that Stage One is, in practice, the most underreported stage: there is no institutional mechanism by which a Stage One subject necessarily comes to the Archive's attention, and most do not. The Archive estimates, based on extrapolation from known deck distribution data, that the true population of Stage One subjects is significantly larger than its documented cohort. It does not have a precise figure. It has noted this gap. The gap is in the file.
