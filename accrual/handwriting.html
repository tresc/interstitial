The Handwriting Phenomenon

Classification: General Use  ·  First documented: Ostroff, 1994
Stage Two (onset); persists through Three
Subject awareness Typically absent
Commission documentation None (public)
Third handwriting Documented once; unexplained

The handwriting phenomenon is the informal name for a feature of Stage Two Accrual first noted by Dr. Yael Ostroff in a 1994 paper and subsequently confirmed across all documented Stage Two subjects for whom pre- and post-transition writing samples exist. The phenomenon is straightforward to describe and has not been explained: at some point during Stage Two, subjects' handwriting changes, without their awareness, to a uniform, regular, upright script that is identical across all documented subjects.

The Commission does not mention this in any public-facing documentation. When the Archive formally raised it in 1995, Commission representatives described it as "a side effect of increased procedural clarity." When the Archive asked what procedural clarity meant in this context, the Commission said the phrase was self-explanatory. The Archive's position is that it is not.

Description of the convergent script

The handwriting that Stage Two subjects converge on has been described by every researcher who has studied it in the same terms: it is the handwriting of a form. It is regular without being mechanical. It is clear. It spaces letters and words consistently. It uses no idiosyncratic letterforms. It would be unremarkable in any administrative context. In any personal context it is wrong in a way that is immediately obvious once you have seen the subject's pre-transition handwriting.

The convergent script is not the Commission's own typeface, which is a printed monospace font used in official documentation. It is a handwritten script that resembles the Commission's institutional tone without being derived from it. The Archive has found no original source for it — no individual whose natural handwriting it matches, no historical script it derives from. It appears to have no origin. It appears fully-formed in each subject who develops it, identical each time.

Subject awareness

Subjects almost universally do not notice the change as it happens. The transition is gradual — occurring over weeks — and subjects who write regularly tend to adapt to their own changing output without registering it as changed. The first documented case of a subject noticing their own transition was Daniel [surname redacted], who described the experience in a personal document as follows:

Recovered document — personal account, 1996 Document 005, p.8
My handwriting changed eight months ago. I didn't notice until I looked at something I'd written before and couldn't recognise it as mine. The new handwriting is fine. It's clear. People can read it easily. I keep thinking I should mind that more than I do.

Daniel's account — the clarity, the legibility, the sense that he should mind this more than he does — is consistent across the small number of subjects who have noticed the change. The Archive's interpretation is that the emotional flattening characteristic of Stage Two extends, in some subjects, to a reduced capacity to respond with appropriate concern to things that should be concerning. This may include the loss of one's own handwriting.

The handwriting in the documents

The most significant implication of the convergent script is its presence in the recovered document collection as a marker. Several documents in the Archive's holdings can be identified as originating from Stage Two subjects — or entities operating in the manner of Stage Two subjects — by their handwriting alone, regardless of authorship attribution. This includes Document 005, in which the handwriting shift is visible across seventeen pages, and several Commission internal documents whose authors have not been identified.

It also includes the closing section of Document 009, which is in Dr. Ostroff's handwriting in its opening and the convergent script in its closing paragraph. Dr. Ostroff has reviewed this document. She has noted that the argument in the convergent-script section is consistent with her own thinking. She has not commented further.

The third handwriting

In Document 008 — a fragment of unknown origin found inside a copy of the Ostroff monograph — the main text is written in an unidentified personal hand. The closing three lines are in the convergent Commission script. Between them, occurring once, is a single word written in a third hand that does not match either the personal hand, the convergent script, or any other sample in the Archive's collection.

The word is Clara.

The Archive has reviewed this against every handwriting sample in its holdings. The third hand has no match. It does not appear to belong to any documented subject, any Commission representative, or any Archive staff member past or present. It appears once, in a document of uncertain provenance, and does not appear again.

The Archive has a theory. The theory is not ready to be published. It is in the file.

Daniel's daughter's name is Clara. Daniel spent the last coherent period of his documented inner life writing her name on the backs of Commission forms. He was in Stage Two when he did this. His handwriting, at that point, was the convergent script. The word "Clara," when he wrote it, was in the convergent script. The word "Clara" in Document 008 is not. The Archive has noted that these two facts are simultaneously true and has not resolved what they mean together.
