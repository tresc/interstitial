The Fold is the terminal event of the Accrual process. It is not a deterioration and does not appear to involve pain. It is, structurally, an event: at some point, without reliable warning, the Stage Three subject ceases to be present in three-dimensional space. The process takes no measurable time. It leaves no physical remains. Witnesses consistently report a brief smell of ozone and, in several documented cases, a sound described as a drawer closing.

The Commission designates this event Completion of Accord and does not classify it as terminal. The Archive designates it The Fold and does. This disagreement has been the subject of formal correspondence on three occasions and informal discussion on many more, without resolution.

The event

The Fold is witnessed in a minority of documented cases — most occur without observers. In those that have been witnessed, accounts are consistent in the following: the subject was present; then they were not. No intermediate state has been reliably documented. No witness has described seeing the subject in the process of disappearing. The transition does not appear to have a middle.

The smell of ozone is present in 84% of witnessed cases. The auditory phenomenon — the sound of a drawer closing — is present in 61%. It is unclear whether the drawer sound is metaphorical (that is, whether witnesses are describing a sound that resembles a drawer, which may or may not be literally what they heard) or literal. The Archive's position is that the distinction may not matter.

The aftermath card

Every documented Fold event is followed, within days, by the appearance of a new card in an existing Conduit Deck. The card always depicts something specific to the subject's life: an object they owned, a place they spent time in, a posture they characteristically held. In several cases the connection has been immediately obvious to those who knew the subject. In several others it has been opaque.

The card is always warm to the touch, which distinguishes it from standard deck cards and from the anthracite piece found in Thorne's room, which was also warm. Players who find the card in their deck consistently report having no memory of it not being there. It has, in their experience, always been in the deck. The Archive has found no way to reconcile this experience with the card's documented post-Fold appearance. It has filed both data points. They do not fit together. They are in the file anyway.

The question of what remains

The central dispute between the Archive and the Commission concerns the fate of the subject following The Fold. The Commission holds that the subject continues to exist within the Substrate's record — that they have been received, in the Commission's language, and that their consciousness persists in the atemporal static field. The Curators describe this as transcendence. The Accelerants describe it as an upgrade.

The Archive's position, stated by Dr. Ostroff in the 1994 monograph and not substantially revised since: the Substrate is a storage medium. A consciousness within the Substrate's record is not experiencing anything. It is stored. The distinction between existing as a static file and not existing is, from the subject's perspective, meaningless. This is not a comforting conclusion. It is the Archive's honest assessment of the available evidence.

Thorne

The disappearance of Dr. Elias Thorne in October 1974 is the earliest documented event consistent with The Fold. Thorne was not, at the time, understood to be in Stage Three — the staging model did not exist yet — but retrospective analysis of the available documentation, including Otto Vess's accounts of Thorne's physical appearance during the transcription period, suggests Stage Three markers were present.

The Commission classifies Thorne as "in process," a designation that appears once in their documentation without elaboration and which the Archive has requested clarification on four times. No clarification has been provided. The Archive notes that "in process" is not the same as "filed." It does not know if the Commission is aware of this distinction or if the Commission considers it meaningful.

The Archive's position on Stage Four as "terminal" is contested by Commission representatives, who note that "terminal" implies an ending and that their documentation does not support this characterisation. The Archive has noted their objection. The Archive has also noted that the subjects in question are no longer present to offer their own characterisation, and that in the absence of such testimony, "terminal" remains the most precise available term. This discussion is ongoing. See talk page.

The cards that remain

The aftermath cards — warm, self-inserting, already-always present in the decks that receive them — are the only physical trace of subjects who have Folded. They are, in the Archive's assessment, the most troubling artefact category in the entire study of Interstitial. Not because they are dangerous. Because they are present. Because they are warm. Because whoever is holding them is holding something that was, recently, a person, and the card does not feel like a memorial. It feels like a message that has not yet been decoded.

The Archive does not know what the message says. The Archive is still here. It is still filing.
