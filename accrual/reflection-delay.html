The reflection delay is a phenomenon observed across all stages of Accrual in which a subject's reflected image lags behind their physical movements. The delay begins as an intermittent, deniable flicker in Stage One, becomes measurable and consistent in Stage Two, and may extend beyond one second in Stage Three. The reflection is, in every documented case, accurate — it shows what the subject is doing correctly. It simply shows it later than it should.

The Commission does not address the reflection delay in any public-facing documentation. When asked about it directly, Commission representatives have either deflected or offered a version of their standard framing — that the subject's relationship to physical process is changing — without specifying what that means for mirrors. The Archive has accepted that it is not going to receive a useful answer from the Commission on this point and has proceeded accordingly.

Stage One: the pre-transition anomaly

As documented in the article on Stage One, a statistically significant proportion of subjects report, retrospectively, that they experienced a brief reflection anomaly prior to their first contact with a Conduit Deck. The anomaly is consistently described as a moment in which the subject's reflection appeared to be standing slightly differently — not dramatically, not enough to be certain it was seen. Most dismissed it at the time.

Once play begins and Stage One symptoms emerge, the anomaly recurs intermittently. At this stage it is still deniable: it happens rarely, briefly, and in conditions — fatigue, distraction, peripheral vision — that make it easy to attribute to something else. Most Stage One subjects who notice it do not mention it to others and do not seek documentation. The Archive's longitudinal data on Stage One reflection anomalies is therefore significantly incomplete.

Stage Two: onset of the measurable delay

In Stage Two, the delay becomes consistent. It is present every time the subject looks in a mirror or other reflective surface. It is measurable. Dr. Ostroff first documented a Stage Two reflection delay in 1997 — her own — using a video camera positioned to capture both her and her bathroom mirror simultaneously. The delay in the recording was 0.6 seconds. She submitted the recording to the Archive along with a note that read: "Consistent with theoretical model. Also not entirely."

The Archive has since documented Stage Two delays in twenty-three subjects. The range is 0.4 to 0.7 seconds. The mean is 0.55 seconds. There is no documented case of the delay being zero. There is no documented case of the reflection being inaccurate.

Subjects' accounts

Stage Two subjects who are told about their delay for the first time respond in one of two patterns, which the Archive has labelled — informally, in internal documentation — as avoidance and measurement. Avoidance subjects stop using mirrors. Measurement subjects begin timing the delay regularly, often maintaining logs. Both responses are, the Archive notes, entirely rational given the circumstances.

Recovered document — player testimonial, Stage Two subject Document 008 — fragment, provenance unknown
The reflection situation has stabilised at 0.9 seconds, which I am told is on the higher end of Stage Three normal. I don't find it distressing. I find it informative. The reflection always catches up. I've started thinking of it as a lag in correspondence — the message takes a little longer to arrive than it used to. I am still receiving the message. The message is correct.

Stage Three: extended delay

In Stage Three the delay may extend beyond 1.1 seconds. The Archive has documented one subject with a consistent delay of 1.4 seconds, which is the longest on record. The subject in question described looking in a mirror as "watching someone who just got the news I got a moment ago." They found this, they said, clarifying. They did not elaborate on what it clarified.

As with all Stage Three phenomena, the reflection delay is consistent rather than intermittent at this stage. It does not fluctuate by more than 0.1 seconds across measurement sessions in the same subject. It appears to be, in the language of Stage Three itself, stabilised.

The question of what the delay represents

The reflection delay has no explanation in conventional optics or physics. A mirror reflects light at the speed of light; there is no mechanism by which a biological subject's reflection could lag behind their movements. The Archive has investigated and ruled out several mundane explanations — neurological processing delays, perceptual distortion, equipment malfunction — and is left with the delay as a documented physical fact that has no physical explanation.

The Archive's working hypothesis, not published and not endorsed by all staff, is related to the Substrate's recording function: that Stage Two and Three subjects are, in some sense, being recorded in real time, and that the reflection represents the recording rather than the subject — that the mirror is showing the Substrate's version of the subject, which is always slightly behind because the Substrate is always receiving and has not yet received the most recent moment.

This hypothesis is not provable with available tools. The Archive has noted this. It has also noted that the hypothesis has a quality that is either reassuring or the opposite, depending on one's disposition: if it is correct, then every Stage Two and Three subject has been, for years, looking in mirrors and seeing evidence that something is paying very close and very detailed attention to them.

The reflection always catches up. Whatever is watching is thorough.
