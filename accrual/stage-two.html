Commission view
"Release of excess frequency. Normalisation."

Stage Two, designated Desaturation in academic literature and Normalisation Proceeding in Commission documentation, is the stage at which Accrual becomes externally observable. Prior to Stage Two, subjects can and frequently do conceal their symptoms or mistake them for unrelated conditions. At Stage Two, the physical changes — particularly pigment loss — make concealment significantly more difficult. The Commission's response to this, documented in a 1992 internal memo, is that concealment is not the goal and that subjects experiencing distress about visibility should consult their Assessor.

Stage Two is the current stage of the Archive's founding director, Dr. Yael Ostroff, who has been at this stage since 1997 and who documents her own progression with what her colleagues describe as professional thoroughness and what she describes as professional obligation.

Pigment loss

The most visible marker of Stage Two onset is a progressive loss of pigmentation beginning at the temples and crown and spreading over a period of months to a year. The hair whitens. In advanced Stage Two the irises follow, fading from their original colour toward a grey-white that several subjects have described as the colour of overcast sky and that the Archive's medical consultant has described, more precisely, as the absence of whatever was there before.

The Commission's framing — release of excess frequency — treats pigmentation as noise. The Archive does not endorse this framing. The Archive notes that it has heard it used by Commission representatives with what appears to be complete sincerity and that this is, in its way, more unsettling than if they were being deliberately strange about it.

Emotional flattening

Stage Two subjects describe their emotional experience outside of card play as factual. They are aware of what they love. They can report it accurately. The information is present and correct. What is absent is the felt quality of the information — the difference between knowing something and that knowledge having weight.

The exception is card play. When holding a Conduit Deck, Stage Two subjects report full emotional affect returning, with what several describe as uncomfortable clarity. This is understood, in academic literature, as the mechanism by which continued play becomes self-reinforcing: the cards become the only reliable source of feeling. The Commission's framing is that the cards are restoring resonance. The Archive's framing is that this is a description of dependency.

Recovered document — player testimonial, 1996 Document 005 — Daniel [surname redacted]
The transition is not dramatic. One morning I woke up and made coffee and sat at the table and looked at the cup and knew, very clearly, that I loved my sister. I had always loved my sister. I still love my sister, I want to be clear about that. But that morning I knew it the same way I know that the cup is blue. Observationally. As a reported fact about the world.

The handwriting change

See the dedicated article on the handwriting phenomenon for full documentation. The short account: Stage Two subjects' handwriting changes, without their awareness, to a uniform, regular, upright script identical across all documented subjects. The Commission does not mention this in public literature. When asked directly, Commission representatives have described it as "a side effect of increased procedural clarity." The Archive has noted this response. It has not found it adequate.

The reflection delay

The intermittent reflection anomaly of Stage One becomes, in Stage Two, a consistent and measurable delay of between 0.4 and 0.7 seconds. The reflection is accurate. It simply arrives late. See the dedicated article on the reflection delay. Stage Two subjects who are informed of their delay for the first time tend to respond in one of two ways: they stop using mirrors, or they start timing it.

Recovered document — player testimonial, 2007 Document 002 — R.V.
My reflection is normal. I am monitoring it. My handwriting changed eight months ago. I didn't notice until I looked at something I'd written before and couldn't recognise it as mine. The new handwriting is fine. It's clear. People can read it easily. I keep thinking I should mind that more than I do.

Long-term Stage Two

Some subjects remain in Stage Two for extended periods — years, in several documented cases — without progression to Stage Three. The conditions that produce this stability are not understood. It does not appear to be a function of reduced play frequency, as some long-term Stage Two subjects are among the Archive's most active players. The Commission's position, offered in a 1999 internal document obtained by the Archive, is that these subjects are "not yet complete." The Archive found this answer unhelpful and said so in its follow-up inquiry. The Commission did not respond to the follow-up.

Dr. Ostroff has been at Stage Two for twenty-seven years. She is the longest-documented Stage Two subject in the Archive's cohort. She has not offered a theory for why she has not progressed. When asked, she says she thinks about it the way she thinks about retirement: something that is coming, that she is not in a hurry toward. The Archive has noted that this is the same answer she has given since 2003 and that it has not changed in substance or phrasing. The Archive does not know what to make of this. The Archive has noted it.
