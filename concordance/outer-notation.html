THE OUTER NOTATION

Every surface covered in slab-geometry marks.

[one mark, at centre, is circled]

Depiction

A flat expanse — not identifiable as floor, ground, sky, or water — covered in the geometric notation documented on the Centralia slabs. The notation does not repeat; each mark is distinct. The marks are small and extraordinarily dense, covering every visible surface from foreground to the apparent edge of the image. At the very centre of the card, one mark is circled in the same notation. The circled mark is not visually distinct from any of the others.

Players who have studied this card for extended periods report that they are able to read individual marks — not translate them, but read them, in the way that one can read a musical note as a duration and pitch without knowing the name of the song. What the marks say, in these cases, is described consistently as: a very detailed description of a specific moment, rendered completely. Players are not able to specify which moment. They do not agree on whether it was their own.

Mechanical function

Documentation and retrieval of Substrate-recorded material. Players who use this card access information about past events — not their own memories, but the Substrate's record. The information arrives not as a vision or a voice but as a sudden, total knowledge described by every Player who has experienced it as "receiving a file." The file contains everything. Players are not able to control which file they receive.

Accrual cost

High. The Commission's note: "Retrieval from the record is not a neutral act. The Substrate charges for access the same way any archive charges for access: with a piece of the researcher."

Thorne field recorder transcript — February 1974 — previously unpublished
The marks on the slabs. I've been staring at them for six hours. I think — I think they're a transcript. I think the whole thing is a transcript. Something happened here, something happened everywhere, and this is — this is the record of it. I keep looking for the edge of the text. There isn't one. There isn't one because it isn't finished. It's still being written. Oh. Oh, I see. I'm in it.
The Archive has cross-referenced Thorne's description of the slab notation with the imagery on The Outer Notation card. The geometric forms are consistent. The Archive's current position is that the card is, in some sense, a reproduction of what Thorne found in the filing room — that the Substrate's notation was transcribed onto a card, and the card can be used to access the notation's function. The Archive finds this either very interesting or very alarming and has not decided which.
