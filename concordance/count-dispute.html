The Count Dispute

Classification: General Use  ·  Documented since 1976  ·  Unresolved
Range documented 31–89 cards
Consistent mean None
Commission position "The deck contains what is present"
Resolution Ongoing. Not expected.

The indeterminate card count of the Conduit Deck is one of the most extensively documented and least explained properties of Interstitial. It is not a matter of counting errors. It is not a matter of cards being hidden or misfiled. It is a fundamental property of the deck that resists enumeration, and it has been consistently reproduced across every counting methodology the Archive has attempted in thirty years of study.

Early documentation

The first recorded instance of count discrepancy appears in a 1976 game log submitted to what was then a loose network of Players, two years after Conduit Deck Zero was completed. The log's author notes, almost as an aside: "Counted the deck before play as usual. Came up with forty-one. Counted again after. Forty-six. Did not add cards. Did not remove cards. Filed for reference."

This observation was noted, set aside, and rediscovered twice before Ostroff incorporated it into her systematic counting methodology in 1992. By that point, enough independent reports had accumulated that the count variability could no longer be attributed to individual error.

The 1992 controlled count

Ostroff's 1992 protocol remains the most rigorous documented attempt to establish a reliable count for a single deck. Three researchers counted the same deck independently, in the same room, simultaneously, using numbered index cards to track each card as it was moved from one pile to another. They counted under observation by two additional witnesses who did not handle the cards.

Results: forty-four, fifty-one, and thirty-eight.

The session was filmed. The Archive has reviewed the footage extensively. No cards enter or leave the frame. No counting errors are visible. Each researcher moves through a stack of cards, places them one by one in a counted pile, arrives at a total. The totals do not match. The footage does not explain why. The footage is in vault B.

Theoretical positions

Commission doctrine

The Commission's 1987 procedural document states: "The deck contains the cards that are present." In a 1994 follow-up inquiry the Archive asked the Commission to expand on this. The Commission responded that the statement was complete. The Archive asked whether "present" referred to physical presence, perceptual presence, or something else. The Commission did not respond to this follow-up. The correspondence is in the file.

The Substrate-alignment hypothesis

Ostroff's preferred model, outlined in her 1994 monograph and not substantially revised: the deck's apparent count at any given moment reflects the Substrate's current assessment of what the player needs rather than a fixed physical inventory. The deck does not have a stable count because it is not a stable object — it is a dynamic interface between the player and the Substrate, and its contents adjust accordingly. This model explains count variability between sessions but does not fully account for simultaneous discrepancies between different observers of the same deck.

The observer-dependent hypothesis

A minority position in the literature, proposed by Archive researcher Dr. Kwame Asante in 2008: the count discrepancy is observer-dependent in the manner of certain quantum phenomena — the act of counting does not reveal a pre-existing number but participates in determining one. Different observers determine different numbers because the determination is partially a function of the observer. This model accounts for simultaneous discrepancies but has been criticised for importing physics frameworks that may not apply.

The Commission's Form 7-C exemption

Notably, the Commission's own procedural documentation exempts deck count discrepancies of "fewer than twelve cards" from mandatory Form 7-C reporting. The Archive has noted two things about this exemption. First: it implies the Commission considers count discrepancies routine up to a gap of eleven. Second: the choice of twelve as the threshold implies the Commission has a specific reason for that number, which it has not shared.

The Archive has asked the Commission about the twelve-card threshold on two separate occasions. The first response was that the threshold was determined by historical data. The second response was that the first response was complete. The Archive has filed both responses. It does not find them adequate. It notes that "not adequate" and "wrong" are not the same thing, and that it is possible the Commission has a perfectly sensible reason for the number twelve and simply will not share it, which is in its own way more unsettling than if the number were arbitrary.
