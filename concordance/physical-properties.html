The physical characteristics of the Conduit Deck cards have been the subject of sustained material analysis since Ostroff first submitted samples to the Dunmore University materials science department in 1990. The analysis has been, in the materials scientists' own words, "consistently inconclusive in ways that are not consistent with normal inconclusiveness." The Archive has quoted this phrase in three publications. It remains the most accurate single-sentence description of the situation.

The cardstock

The cards are printed on heavy grey cardstock of a weight and texture that has no exact commercial equivalent. Fibre analysis suggests a composition of cotton, linen, and at least one material that the Archive's materials consultant describes as "present in the analysis but not identifiable as a known substance." The cards do not yellow, crack, or show wear consistent with use. Decks that are documented as having been in continuous use for decades show no degradation. Conduit Deck Zero — if the deck held by the Archive's collection is indeed the original — shows no aging consistent with a document produced in 1974.

The imagery

Each card bears a single image executed in a style resembling detailed charcoal work — dense, precise, monochromatic. The images are consistent across copies of the same card in different decks, with minor variations that have been catalogued in the Concordance entries. Photographic reproduction of the images is possible but the Archive has noted that photographs of the cards carry none of the peripheral animation property — they are inert in a way the cards themselves are not.

The peripheral animation — the property by which images appear to shift when observed out of direct focus — has been documented under controlled conditions across seventeen subjects. It is not present in photographs or high-resolution scans of the cards. It is present only when observing a physical card. Its mechanism is unknown. The Commission does not address it. Several Players report never having noticed it, which the Archive considers more unsettling than if everyone noticed it.

Mechanical function

Playing a card against a surface — the physical act of placing it — produces a localised alteration of probability and physical reality. The effect is specific to the card played, consistent across play instances, and scales with the magnitude of change required: small alterations carry low Accrual cost, large ones carry high cost.

Ostroff's micro-fissure model, from the 1994 monograph: each card play introduces a small structural impossibility into physical spacetime. The local reality attempts to correct for this impossibility — to heal the fissure — and the healing process produces the observed effect. The deck is, in this model, entirely parasitic: it uses the player's Substrate-adjacent energy as the fuel for localised reality correction. Playing a card does not create anything. It breaks something small, and reality fixes it, and the fixing is what the player experiences as the card's effect.

Temperature and post-Fold cards

Standard deck cards are cold to the touch — noticeably so, in a way that Players consistently remark on and that has been measured at approximately 2–3°C below ambient temperature regardless of handling. The cold does not warm with prolonged holding. It is persistent.

Cards added to a deck through a Fold event are warm — approximately 3–4°C above ambient. This warmth also persists with handling and does not fade over time. The temperature differential is the Archive's primary method of identifying post-Fold additions, and is the basis for the Archive's ongoing register of known Fold events: each post-Fold card, identified by warmth, represents a subject who has Folded and been incorporated into the deck.

The Archive holds seven confirmed warm cards across its collection. Seven subjects, therefore, are in someone's deck. The Archive does not find this easier to think about with time.

The Archive submitted a warm card for thermal imaging in 2006 to determine whether the warmth had an identifiable physical source — a chemical reaction, a residual biological process, anything. The thermal imaging showed a consistent heat signature with no identifiable source. The card was warm the way a thing is warm when something warm was recently inside it. The imaging report uses exactly this phrase. The Archive's materials consultant later said she wished she had worded it differently. The Archive told her the wording was correct.
