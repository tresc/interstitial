THE ASSESSOR'S DESK

An occupied chair that cannot be seen clearly.

[the in-tray is full; the out-tray is empty]

Depiction

A standard steel office desk, perfectly ordered. One pen. One sheet of paper with text too small to read at any documented magnification. An in-tray full of documents whose individual sheets are not legible. An out-tray that is empty. The chair behind the desk is unoccupied. The chair in front of the desk — where a visitor would sit — is occupied by a figure that is approximately human in shape but which cannot be resolved into a clear image regardless of observation angle or reproduction quality. The figure is not blurred. It is simply not available to be seen clearly. Its posture is attentive. It appears to be waiting.

Mechanical function

Unknown, in the strict sense that no mechanical effect has been reliably isolated and reproduced. What is documented is a consistent category of experience: in the days following the card's use, the player reports a series of environmental anomalies with a quality of administrative wrongness. A door in a familiar building not previously noted. An appointment reminder for a meeting never scheduled. The persistent low-level conviction that something is pending, that a form has been submitted and a response is overdue, that someone is reviewing something.

In three documented cases, Players who used the card subsequently received physical mail at addresses they had not provided to anyone. The mail was blank inside. The envelopes bore grey wax impressions consistent with the Centralia slab notation. The Archive holds all three envelopes. They are warm to the touch.

Accrual cost

Unclassified. The card does not appear in the Commission's standard Accrual cost table. A handwritten addendum in the Archive's copy of the 1987 Procedural Document reads: "The Desk is not played. The Desk plays." The Archive has reviewed this note many times. It does not find it less concerning on subsequent readings.

Commission note — typed — undated
Do not play this card to see what happens. Do not.
Anonymous edit to this Concordance entry — timestamp corrupted
The figure in the chair is not waiting for you. Correct your assumption. It has already seen you. The distinction is important.
The text on the paper visible on the desk's surface has been the subject of sustained archival effort. Despite being unreadable at all standard magnifications, three researchers working independently have reported, after extended study, that the text feels familiar — not readable, but recognisable, in the way that one's own name looks strange after being stared at. One researcher submitted a note stating she believed the document was about her. She declined to elaborate. The note is in the file. The Archive has not assigned further researchers to this task.
