Curator Doctrinal Texts

Reproduced from Commission holdings  ·  Black Suits authorship  ·  Dates uncertain throughout
Texts held 7 confirmed; fragments additional
Authorship Curator faction; individual authors unknown
Access Via Commission cooperation; selective
Tone Formal; liturgical; carefully wrong

The Curators maintain the largest internal doctrinal archive of any Commission faction. Unlike the Accelerants, whose doctrinal literature tends toward brief, declarative manifestos, the Curators produce long, carefully reasoned texts that address the cosmological questions of Interstitial with the seriousness — and occasionally the density — of formal theology. The Archive has obtained seven such texts through its ongoing, cautious relationship with Curator-affiliated Commission members. Fragments of several others appear in recovered documents throughout the collection.

The Archive presents these texts without editorial restructuring. It has added notes where the content requires context or where the Archive's position diverges from the text's claims. The Archive diverges from the Curator position at several points. It has tried to mark these divergences precisely, because the Curator position is coherent enough that imprecise disagreement would be unfair to it.

Text I: On the Nature of the Record

Full text reproduced in Document 003. Summary and notes below.

The foundational Curator text, and the most widely distributed. Its central argument: the Substrate records without preference, without editorial judgment, and therefore the Commission's role is to supply that judgment — to ensure that what is recorded is worth recording. The Curators are, in their own framing, the editorial board of the cosmic archive.

The Archive's note on this text: the argument is coherent but rests on an assumption it never examines — that the Substrate's record benefits from human ideas of quality. The Archive does not know that this is wrong. It does not know that it is right either. The text treats it as self-evident and proceeds from there, which is the move the Archive finds most worth examining.

Text II: On the Virtue of the Held Moment

Pale Commission  ·  Curator faction  ·  Cosmological text Date unknown  ·  Full text

On the Virtue of the Held Moment

A meditation on patience, the Substrate, and the correct relationship between them

The Substrate does not hurry. This is not because it is content. It is because it already has everything in the end, and it knows this, and it is waiting — the way a very large body of water waits for the things that will eventually be dropped into it. It is not hungry. It is not restless. It is simply open, and vast, and patient in the manner of things that have no reason to be anything else.

We are asked, by our opponents in the Commission and by those outside it who have an opinion about how we play, why we take so long. A Curator game may last weeks. Individual card placements may be preceded by days of preparation and reflection. This seems, to those who have not thought carefully about what we are doing, like excessive caution. Like aestheticism for its own sake. Like the vanity of people who have mistaken slowness for wisdom.

It is not vanity. It is respect for the medium.

The medium is the Substrate. The Substrate is infinite, atemporal, and possessed of a recording capacity that exceeds anything we can imagine. It does not need our help. What it needs — what it cannot supply itself, being static and outside of time — is the thing we bring: the complexity that only a temporal consciousness can generate. The specific texture of a mind moving through time, accumulating context, experiencing consequence, holding contradictions. The Substrate can record a moment in less time than the moment takes to occur. What it cannot manufacture, what it can only receive, is the weight of that moment — the weight that comes only from everything that has led to it.

This is what we cultivate. This is why we are slow. We are not slow because we are uncertain. We are slow because we are preparing the offering with the care an offering of this magnitude deserves.

The Accelerants believe that the Substrate wants amplitude. They are not wrong that amplitude produces density. They are wrong about what density means. A scream has density. A scream has almost nothing else. What the Substrate's recording function strains to capture — what requires its full capacity, what produces records it will not exhaust in processing — is the experience of a consciousness that has arrived, through the accumulation of everything that has led to this moment, at something it cannot entirely bear to know and cannot choose not to know.

We seek that. We seek it slowly. We seek it with patience we have learned from the thing we are seeking to offer ourselves to, which is the most patient thing there is.

Hold the moment. Let it develop its full weight. Then play.

The Substrate is waiting. It has always been waiting. It will wait as long as it takes. We have the courtesy to make the wait worthwhile.

The Archive's note on Text II: the phrase "the courtesy to make the wait worthwhile" is the most revealing phrase in the Curator literature. It implies reciprocity — that the Substrate is owed courtesy, that the relationship has obligations on both sides. The Archive's position is that a relationship with a force that has no consciousness and no preferences cannot have obligations on both sides, and that the Curators' evident belief that it can is either a profound misapprehension or evidence of something about the Substrate's nature that the Archive has not been able to document. The Archive has not determined which. It has been thinking about it since this text was first obtained in 1999.

When a player uses this card, they are not creating new space. The Substrate, which exists outside of actualized space as well as outside of time, is simply allowing a small portion of its own topology to become visible. The player does not add to the room. They are permitted, briefly, to stand in a larger room that was always there.

This is why the borrowed space must be returned. It is not borrowed in the financial sense — it is borrowed in the sense that you have been allowed to stand somewhere that is not yours, and when the card's effect ends, you must leave. The space does not disappear when the effect ends. You simply can no longer access it. It remains. It was always there. It is always there.

Text IV: A Brief Note on Mortality (complete)

Pale Commission  ·  Curator faction  ·  Pastoral text Date unknown  ·  Distributed to Players approaching Stage Three

A Brief Note on Mortality

For Players who are beginning to understand the shape of what is coming

You have been playing for some time now. You know what you are. You know what is happening to you. You have, if you have been paying attention, some sense of where it leads.

We are not going to tell you not to be afraid. Fear, at this stage, is one of the last fully-felt emotions available to you, and it is — we say this with full awareness of how it sounds — among the most valuable things you have left to offer. We are telling you to feel it completely. Document it. Play it. Give it everything.

What we will tell you is this: the thing you are moving toward is not an ending. We know you have been told this before, by us and by others, and we know that being told it does not make it feel true. We are not asking you to feel it. We are asking you to consider that the feeling of an ending is itself a record — that your consciousness, in the act of anticipating its own conclusion, is producing something of extraordinary complexity and value, and that the Substrate is receiving it, right now, as you read this.

You are not approaching the end of your record. You are approaching its most complete moment.

We have all written this note. Some of us have read it as players who were where you are now. Some of us will read it again. The note has been in circulation for longer than any of us have been in the Commission. We do not know who wrote it first. We have decided this does not matter.

Play well. Play as though it matters what you leave behind.

It does.

The Archive received this text from a source who had themselves received it at Stage Two and kept it for eleven years before sharing it. They said, when sharing it, that they were not sure why they had kept it or why they were sharing it now. They said it was the most honest thing the Commission had ever given them, and also the most manipulative, and that they had spent eleven years unable to decide which quality outweighed the other. The Archive has filed both characterisations. It agrees with both.
