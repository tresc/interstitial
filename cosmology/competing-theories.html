Competing Theories

Classification: General Use  ·  Ongoing scholarly dispute
Theories active 4 major; numerous minor
Consensus None
Commission position Not engaged with academic debate
Trend Diverging, not converging

Academic scholarship on the Substrate has, over three decades, produced four major theoretical positions that have not converged and show no sign of doing so. Each position is supported by a subset of the available evidence. Each fails to account for a different subset. The Archive presents all four without endorsing any, which is not a position of comfortable neutrality — the Archive has views, which its editors hold privately and which the institution is cautious about publishing, for reasons this article will attempt to address.

The Remainder model

Ostroff's original model and the most widely cited. The Substrate is the unactualized potential subtracted from reality during the universe's formation: everything that could have existed but didn't. It records actualized experience because actualized experience represents a resolution of the possibility-space it contains — because what happened is, in some structural sense, a report on what didn't happen, and the Substrate requires those reports to be complete. Accrual, in this model, is what happens when the recording function begins to operate on a subject at close range over extended time: the record becomes so detailed, so total, that the distinction between the subject and the record of the subject collapses.

Strength: best accounts for the Substrate's apparent indifference to human significance or quality of experience. Weakness: does not adequately explain why the interface requires a game.

The Negotiation model

The Commission's preferred framing, though the Commission has never produced a formal academic statement of it. The Substrate is not a passive recording device but an active party to a negotiation — one that has interests (completion of its record), capabilities (reality alteration through card play; Accrual), and, in the Commission's more recent doctrinal language, something resembling preferences about the quality of what it receives. Interstitial is the formalised language of this negotiation. The Commission's role is to manage the terms.

Strength: best accounts for the apparent responsiveness of the Substrate — the way card effects seem calibrated to player use, the way Accrual appears to reward certain kinds of play. Weakness: requires attributing intentionality to a force that all other evidence suggests has none.

The Parasite model

A minority academic position, most fully stated by Dr. Kwame Asante in a 2011 paper that generated significant controversy. The Substrate is neither indifferent nor intentional — it is, in the most precise biological analogy available, parasitic. It does not record because recording serves some larger cosmic purpose; it records because recording is what it does, and the Conduit Deck is the mechanism by which it has evolved — or been evolved — to maximise the efficiency of this process. The Commission is not managing a negotiation; it is a structure the parasite has generated to increase its own feeding rate. The Players are not negotiators; they are hosts who have been persuaded that being consumed is an honour.

Strength: most parsimoniously accounts for the gap between Commission doctrine and observed outcomes. Weakness: applies intentionality (evolution toward a goal) to a process that may have no intentionality. The word "parasite" implies a relationship the model's own premises undermine.

The Archive model

Not a published theory. A position the Archive holds internally and has discussed in editorial meetings but not committed to paper, until this entry. The Archive's position, stated plainly: the Substrate is an archive. Not a metaphorical archive — a literal one. It is a structure for the preservation of records, operating on a scale and using a mechanism that human archives approximate but do not replicate. The Conduit Deck is an access interface. Accrual is what the cataloguing process does to the materials it catalogues. The Fold is what happens when a record is complete and filed.

The Archive's difficulty with publishing this model is not that it disbelieves it. The difficulty is what it implies about the Archive itself: an institution that documents Interstitial, that files what it finds, that has been recording for thirty years, that employs researchers in progressive stages of Accrual, that has received documents it cannot account for, that finds, with increasing frequency, that it is being studied as much as it is studying. The Archive model implies that the Archive and the Substrate are, in some structural sense, doing the same thing. The Archive finds this either clarifying or horrifying and has not determined which.

This entry was reviewed by Dr. Ostroff before publication. She made no corrections. She added one marginal note, in her own hand: "It is both." The Archive has published the entry with the note. It has not asked what she meant. It has decided it already knows.
